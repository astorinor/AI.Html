Edgar Allan Poe

Early Life

Edgar Allan Poe was born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts. Orphaned at a young age, he was taken in by John and Frances Allan but had a troubled relationship with his foster father.

Poe briefly attended the University of Virginia but left due to financial difficulties. He later joined the U.S. Army and eventually turned to writing as his primary pursuit.

Literary Career

Poe is best known for his macabre and gothic tales, including "The Tell-Tale Heart" and "The Fall of the House of Usher." His poem "The Raven" brought him widespread recognition.

Despite his literary achievements, Poe struggled financially. He died under mysterious circumstances in 1849, leaving behind a lasting legacy in American literature.

Personal Life

Poe married his cousin Virginia Clemm in 1836, and their relationship was marked by both love and tragedy. Virginia’s illness deeply affected Poe and influenced his darker writings.

He was known for his erratic behavior and struggles with alcoholism, which further contributed to his personal and professional difficulties.
